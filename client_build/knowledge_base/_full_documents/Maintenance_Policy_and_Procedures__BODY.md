## Introduction
The Maintenance Department plays a critical role in ensuring the uninterrupted, efficient, and safe operation of the wheat milling plant. Through the implementation of world-class maintenance practices, the department supports plant reliability, product quality, cost efficiency, and compliance with regulatory standards.
This manual provides a comprehensive set of policies and procedures for the maintenance department. It outlines the strategies, responsibilities, processes, and standards that govern preventive and corrective maintenance activities, mechanical workshop operations, contractor management, and collaboration on projects and automation. It also emphasizes the systematic use of the SAP Plant Maintenance module to track assets, plan and schedule work, monitor performance, and maintain historical records.
By aligning maintenance activities with global best practices, this manual aims to reduce equipment downtime, extend asset life, ensure the safety of personnel and equipment, and contribute to overall business continuity and competitiveness.

## Purpose of the Maintenance Manual
The primary purpose of this manual is to establish a consistent, standardized, and effective framework for managing all maintenance functions within the wheat milling plant. It serves as:

- A reference guide for maintenance personnel and other departments interacting with maintenance.

- A tool to promote compliance with internal policies and external regulatory requirements.

- A means to integrate best practices with the existing SAP system for improved tracking, planning, and decision-making.

- A mechanism for continuous improvement by enabling audits, performance reviews, and training based on documented procedures.
The manual ensures that all activities—from preventive and corrective maintenance to workshop operations, contractor engagement, and project collaboration—are performed in a structured, safe, and efficient manner.

## Scope of the Manual
This manual applies to all maintenance-related activities at the wheat milling plant. Specifically, it covers the following areas:

- Preventive Maintenance (PM): Planning and execution of scheduled inspections and servicing activities to avoid breakdowns and ensure equipment reliability.

- Corrective Maintenance (CM): Timely repair and restoration of equipment following unexpected failures or malfunctions.

- Mechanical Workshop Operations: In-house machining, grinding, and repair works, especially cylinder regrinding and fabrication jobs.

- Contractor Management: Supervision and coordination of external vendors, especially for specialized services like equipment calibration and inspections.

- Collaboration with Projects and Automation Department: Participation in technical feasibility studies, design input, equipment specification, and commissioning support for new installations and automation initiatives.

- SAP Plant Maintenance (SAP PM): Utilization of SAP for notification management, work order processing, spare parts management, preventive schedules, historical tracking, and reporting.
This manual applies to all maintenance personnel, including planners, engineers, technicians, supervisors, and relevant support staff. It also serves as a guide for other departments to understand and align with maintenance policies where coordination is required.

## Maintenance Management System
Introduction and Background of the Section
A robust Maintenance Management System (MMS) forms the foundation of efficient, safe, and cost-effective plant operations. At Arabian Mills, the MMS is designed in alignment with international standards including ISO 55000 (Asset Management) and ISO 14224 (Reliability and Maintenance Data for Equipment), and is fully integrated with the SAP Plant Maintenance (PM) module. This ensures consistency, traceability, and continuous improvement across all maintenance functions.
The system provides a structured framework to identify, prioritize, plan, execute, and monitor maintenance tasks, supported by real-time data and analytics. By utilizing SAP PM, the organization ensures that all maintenance work—from preventive servicing to unplanned corrective repairs and calibration activities—is governed by well-defined processes that are both auditable and transparent.
Arabian Mills’ maintenance strategy embraces principles to ensure equipment uptime, optimal resource utilization, safety compliance, and alignment with production goals. The system supports interdepartmental collaboration, particularly with the Projects and Automation department, and ensures that contractor-managed calibration and external maintenance activities are tracked and standardized.

## Preventive Maintenance
Introduction and Background of the Section
Preventive Maintenance (PM) ensures the proactive servicing of equipment to prevent unexpected failures and maintain operational continuity. At Arabian Mills, PM activities are scheduled weekly and cover all critical milling and packaging equipment. These activities are driven by a structured monthly plan, executed without a full plant shutdown, and supported by SAP Plant Maintenance (SAP PM).
The PM approach is aligned with ISO 14224, Total Productive Maintenance (TPM), and The Society for Maintenance & Reliability Professionals (SMRP) best practices. It includes inspections, adjustments, lubrication, and early detection of anomalies. The system is supported by an in-house planning process and avoids complete shutdowns by staggering tasks through weekdays.
- Preventive maintenance shall be carried out for all critical and semi-critical assets at frequencies determined by equipment manufacturer guidelines, operational data, and maintenance history, with clearly assigned days for specific areas.

- Preventive maintenance shall be prioritized based on equipment criticality and must not be deferred without formal approval.

- Any deviation from the approved preventive maintenance schedule must be presented in the monthly S&OP meeting and authorized by the S&OP Core Team. The reason for the deviation must be documented in the SAP PM module.

- All PM activities shall be recorded and tracked in the SAP Plant Maintenance system.

- PM shall be executed in operating conditions unless safety requires isolation.

- Technicians shall document findings, including deviations and potential failures, in SAP for follow-up action.

- KPIs such as PM compliance, overdue PMs, and equipment downtime trends shall be monitored monthly and reviewed quarterly.

## Corrective Maintenance
Introduction and Background of the Section
Corrective Maintenance (CM) refers to reactive maintenance activities carried out to restore equipment to its operational condition following an unexpected failure, anomaly, or breakdown. At Arabian Mills, corrective maintenance is executed under a structured and traceable system that leverages the SAP Plant Maintenance (SAP PM) module to ensure accountability, timeliness, and cost control.
While preventive maintenance aims to avoid breakdowns, corrective maintenance is critical for addressing unforeseen events promptly and safely. A disciplined CM system ensures minimum disruption to production and allows valuable failure data to be captured for future improvements.
CM at Arabian Mills is based on the principles of ISO 14224, TPM pillar for Breakdown Maintenance, and Reliability-Centered Maintenance (RCM) to ensure quality and continuous improvement.
- All equipment failures or abnormal observations must be immediately reported and documented via SAP PM notifications.

- No corrective action shall be taken unless a work order is generated and approved, except for emergency situations.

- Each breakdown must be logged with failure mode, timestamp, equipment ID, symptoms, and operating condition to enable later Root Cause Analysis (RCA).

- Work order priorities shall be assigned based on safety, production impact, and cost implications, following the predefined priority matrix.

- Safety, hygiene, and product quality standards must be upheld during all CM activities.

- Root cause data from corrective maintenance shall be analyzed periodically to reduce recurring failures.

- Emergency CM interventions shall follow an expedited SAP workflow for logging, resolution, and analysis.

- All CM activities, material usage, and technician hours must be recorded in SAP. Data will be reviewed periodically to identify recurring faults and performance trends.

Corrective Maintenance Priority Matrix
Here is a Corrective Maintenance Priority Matrix for a wheat milling and animal feeds plant, using Safety, Production Impact, and Cost Implications as the three main criteria. The matrix helps determine the priority level for corrective maintenance work orders:

| Priority Level | Safety Impact | Production Impact | Cost Implication | Response Time | Example |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| P1 – Emergency | High risk to personnel or plant safety | Complete production stoppage or major process loss | High risk of significant equipment damage or regulatory penalty | Immediate (within 1 hour) | Fire hazard, exposed live wires, breakdown of main wheat mill drive motor |
| P2 – Urgent | Moderate safety concern (potential to escalate) | Partial line stoppage or major bottleneck | Significant loss if delayed but not catastrophic | Within 4 hours | Sifter blockage, pneumatic line rupture, elevator belt damage |
| P3 – High | No immediate safety risk, but recurring failure | Quality deviation or throughput reduction | Costly if ignored or repeated failure history | Within 24 hours | Vibrating roller mill, abnormal bearing noise, airlock motor overheating |
| P4 – Medium | Minor concern, isolated to a safe condition | No immediate production loss | Minor cost or easily controlled workaround | Within 3 working days | Faulty sensor on a redundant system, misalignment of chute |
| P5 – Low | No safety or production risk | No disruption to operations | Low value maintenance or cosmetic issue | As scheduled / backlog item | Paint touch-up, loose covers, non-functional indicator lamp on control panel |
Priority Assignment Guidelines

- P1: Must be escalated immediately to the Maintenance Manager.

- P2: Reviewed by the Production Maintenance Manager and escalated for resolution

- P3: Reviewed daily during maintenance planning by the maintenance supervisor; scheduled within 24 hours.

- P4: Bundled with planned maintenance or scheduled during low load.

- P5: Added to the backlog list and considered in weekly planning meetings.
Responsibility for Assignment

- Initial Notification: Technician or Operator

- Priority Review and Assignment: Maintenance Planner, with validation by Supervisor or Maintenance Manager based on defined criteria.

## Budgeting
Introduction and Background of the Section
Effective budgeting is essential to ensure that the Maintenance Department at Arabian Mills is adequately resourced for executing preventive, corrective, and reliability-centered activities throughout the year. A structured budgeting process enables cost control, supports capital and operational planning, and ensures alignment with corporate financial goals.
The budgeting process is built upon historical maintenance data, projected workloads, equipment reliability trends, and input from SAP Plant Maintenance. It involves collaboration among maintenance, finance, and procurement functions, and ensures that sufficient provisions are made for spares, contractor services, and unplanned failures. Budget performance is reviewed monthly using SAP-generated variance analysis reports.
- The maintenance budget shall be prepared annually, incorporating inputs from all relevant maintenance functions.

- This maintenance budget serves as a subcomponent of the company-wide consolidated budget, which is finalized by the Finance Department.

- The SAP system shall be used to extract historical maintenance cost data, forecast needs, track budget consumption, and perform variance analysis.

- All cost components—including spares, manpower, contractor services, and capital maintenance—shall be forecasted systematically.

- Variances between actual and budgeted expenses shall be reviewed monthly, with corrective actions taken if required.

- Budget revisions, if necessary, must be formally approved and documented.

## Spare Parts Management
Introduction and Background of the Section
Spare parts management is vital to ensure equipment reliability and timely maintenance execution. At Arabian Mills, the spare parts system is designed to support both preventive and corrective maintenance with readily available, correctly specified, and cost-effective parts. The system is governed by best practices in inventory control and is fully integrated with the SAP MM and PM modules.
Spare parts are categorized based on criticality, usage frequency, and lead time. Reorder points, minimum and maximum stock levels, and inventory movement histories are tracked digitally. The Stores and Maintenance teams collaborate to ensure high availability of critical components while minimizing excess inventory and procurement delays.
- All spare parts must be classified and maintained in the SAP MM module with clearly defined specifications, reorder levels, and criticality tags.

- Spare parts shall be categorized as critical, fast-moving, or slow-moving based on usage frequency, lead time, and impact on production downtime:
  o Critical spares: Required to avoid significant production loss or safety risk; high availability must be ensured.
  o Slow-moving spares: Used infrequently; reviewed periodically to optimize stock levels and avoid overstocking or obsolescence.

- Only SAP-authorized requisitions and issues are allowed for all maintenance work orders.

- Any emergency purchases must be justified by a breakdown notification or safety risk, approved by the Maintenance Director, and processed through an expedited SAP PO flow.

- Periodic reconciliation, consumption tracking, and obsolete stock reviews must be conducted, and Material Requirements Planning (MRP) shall be run through SAP to propose purchase actions based on stock policies.

- Adjustments to stock levels or planning parameters must be approved by the Maintenance Director based on documented justification.

- Vendors for external spares or services shall be prequalified and linked in the SAP supplier database.

Spare Parts Categorization
In a wheat milling and animal feeds plant, spare parts can be effectively categorized using a triangular approach based on:
1. Criticality – impact of part failure on safety, production, and compliance
2. Usage Frequency – how often the part is consumed or replaced
3. Lead Time – time required to procure the part (especially from OEMs abroad)
1. Categorization by Criticality

| Category | Criteria | Examples (Wheat Mill / Feed Plant) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Critical | **- Causes full line stoppage or safety hazard**<br>
- - No redundancy | Main drive motors (roller mills, pellet press), PLC modules, gearboxes |
| Semi-Critical | **- Causes quality issues or partial downtime**<br>
- - Workaround possible | Plansifter sieve frames, feeder belts, VFD units |
| Non-Critical | - No significant impact on production or safety | Panel indicators, control switch covers, inspection windows |
2. Categorization by Usage Frequency

| Category | Criteria | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Fast-Moving | **Frequently consumed parts**<br>
- Used weekly or monthly | Bearings (bucket elevators, roller mills), V-belts, pneumatic fittings |
| Slow-Moving | **Used infrequently**<br>
- Every 6–12 months | Shaft couplings, die clamps (pellet press), spare pulleys |
| Non-Moving | **Rarely or never used**<br>
- No consumption in past year | Obsolete HMI panels, discontinued sensor models |
3. Categorization by Lead Time

| Category | Criteria | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Short Lead Time | Locally available, standard stock | Grease nipples, fasteners, seals, pneumatic tubing |
| Medium Lead Time | Regionally sourced or off-the-shelf OEM parts | Control relays, timing belts, vibration sensors |
| Long Lead Time | Imported/customized/OEM-exclusive parts | Cylinder shells, die rings (pellet press), imported PLC I/O modules |

## External Service Provider
Introduction and Background of the Section
External Service Providers (ESPs) play a key role in supporting the maintenance department at Arabian Mills by delivering specialized services that cannot be performed in-house due to technical complexity, regulatory requirements, or equipment constraints. These services include but are not limited to: instrument calibration, certification inspections, specialized mechanical repairs, and non-destructive testing (NDT).
Arabian Mills uses a structured and transparent process to engage, monitor, and evaluate external contractors. All ESP engagements are integrated with SAP for requisitioning, tracking, performance documentation, and invoice reconciliation. This ensures service quality, timely delivery, and cost control.
This system is aligned with ISO 9001 (Quality Management Systems), ISO 17025 (Calibration Services Competency), and internal safety and reliability protocols. The objective is to ensure that external interventions complement internal maintenance programs without disrupting plant operations or safety.
- Only prequalified and approved external service providers will be engaged for maintenance-related activities.

- All engagements must be based on a formal contract or approved work order, with a clear Scope of Work (SOW) documented and maintained in SAP.

- All engagements must be formalized through SAP purchase requisitions (PR) and purchase orders (PO), linked to maintenance notifications or work orders.

- Safety, confidentiality, and regulatory compliance requirements must be explicitly communicated and enforced.

- Each completed job shall be followed by a performance assessment based on adherence to timelines, quality of work, and documentation standards.

- Invoice processing will follow a structured review, approval, and recording workflow. If budget is not available, the work shall not proceed without express written approval from the Maintenance Director and Finance Department

## Maintenance Planning and Procedures
Introduction and Background of the Section
Effective maintenance planning and procedures are essential for ensuring plant reliability, equipment availability, and operational efficiency. In a wheat milling and animal feeds plant, where production is continuous and highly interdependent, maintenance activities must be meticulously coordinated to minimize downtime and disruptions.
The planning process integrates asset hierarchy, equipment criticality, workload estimation, resource availability, and material readiness, using the SAP Plant Maintenance (PM) module to ensure transparency, consistency, and traceability. The procedures laid out in this section are aligned with ISO 55000 Asset Management, TPM (Total Productive Maintenance), and best practices from Society for Maintenance & Reliability Professionals (SMRP).
This section provides a systematic approach to maintenance scheduling and execution—ensuring that preventive, corrective, and project-related maintenance is strategically distributed across time, resources, and plant availability.
Arabian Mills is committed to ensuring that all maintenance activities—whether preventive, corrective, predictive, or emergency—are planned, executed, monitored, and continuously improved through a structured and data-driven system. The maintenance planning and procedures function serves as the operational backbone that transforms strategic asset care goals into executable work packages that deliver reliability, safety, and cost control.
Accordingly, the following policy directives shall apply:
1. Planned Maintenance as the Norm
  o All maintenance tasks shall be pre-planned and scheduled to the extent possible. Reactive or unplanned work shall be minimized and monitored through KPIs.
  o A monthly maintenance planning cycle shall be followed, aligning maintenance tasks with operational priorities and resource availability.
2. Integration with SAP PM
  o The SAP Plant Maintenance (SAP PM) module shall be the sole system for planning, tracking, and analyzing maintenance work.
  o All notifications, work orders, task lists, permits, spare part requirements, and performance data shall be generated, processed, and closed in SAP to ensure full traceability and audit readiness.
3. Use of Work Orders for All Activities
  o No maintenance task—routine or emergency—shall be executed without a work order issued through SAP.
  o Each work order shall clearly define scope, responsible technician, parts required, safety documents, expected completion date, and feedback mechanism.
4. Checklist-Driven Execution
  o All preventive maintenance and equipment inspections shall be executed using predefined checklists approved by engineering and continuously updated based on field feedback.
  o Checklists shall be embedded in SAP task lists and serve as the basis for work order feedback.
5. Inspection, Safety, and Compliance Integration
  o Equipment inspection activities shall be aligned with safety, food safety, and quality compliance requirements, and shall be fully documented.
  o Critical inspections shall trigger escalations if deviations are found, including immediate creation of corrective work orders.
6. Emergency Work Governance
  o Emergency maintenance shall follow a defined response procedure including notification, temporary mitigation, root cause investigation, and post-incident reporting in SAP.
  o Emergency work must be formally authorized and retroactively documented through SAP work order issuance and closure.
7. Planning Roles and Responsibilities
  o Maintenance Planners shall be responsible for converting notifications into executable schedules, coordinating with production, and aligning spares and manpower.
  o Maintenance Supervisors shall ensure that executed work is compliant, feedback is recorded, and deviations are escalated.
  o The SAP PM Administrator shall ensure data accuracy, user access, and reporting.
  o Directors and Managers shall use system-generated reports to improve planning assumptions, optimize intervals, and eliminate chronic failures.
This policy ensures that the Maintenance Department at Arabian Mills operates with discipline, foresight, and system accountability, fully aligned with reliability, safety, and operational performance goals.

## iv	Lockout/Tagout (LOTO)
Introduction and Background of the Section
Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) is a globally recognized safety practice used to control hazardous energy during the servicing and maintenance of machinery. Its purpose is to prevent the accidental startup or release of stored energy that could cause injury to personnel or damage to equipment.
At present, Arabian Mills relies on informal electrical isolation performed at the electrical panel. However, there is no structured LOTO program, no standardized procedures, and no tagging or verification steps. This exposes technicians to significant safety risks during mechanical and electrical maintenance.
Adopting a formal LOTO program is not only a compliance measure but a critical step toward building a safety-first maintenance culture. It will cover electrical, mechanical, pneumatic, hydraulic, thermal, and chemical energy sources, and apply to all departments and contractors performing maintenance activities.
Arabian Mills shall implement a formal Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) program to protect personnel from unexpected equipment startup or hazardous energy release during maintenance and servicing.
The LOTO system shall be:

- Mandatory for all maintenance activities where there is risk of stored or residual energy

- Aligned with international safety standards, including OSHA 29 CFR 1910.147

- Applied consistently to electrical, mechanical, pneumatic, and hydraulic systems

- Standardized through written procedures, lockout devices, padlocks, and tags

- Supervised by authorized personnel with clearly defined roles and responsibilities

- Audited regularly for compliance, effectiveness, and continuous improvement
Failure to follow LOTO procedures shall be treated as a serious safety violation, subject to disciplinary action.

Continuous Improvement in the maintenance department should be supported by:

- Structured Root Cause Analysis (RCA)

- Use of data-driven KPIs (as defined in Section 1.11)

- Require formal Root Cause Analysis for recurring failures, high downtime events, and safety-critical incidents

## Root Cause Analysis
Introduction and Background
Root Cause Analysis is a systematic method used to identify the origin of problems or failures and determine corrective actions that prevent recurrence. Without structured RCA, the same issues are likely to repeat, leading to:

- Increased reactive maintenance

- Higher operational risk

- Inefficient use of manpower and spares
This section establishes a formal RCA system using global best practices, including:

- Clear RCA triggers based on KPIs

- Use of structured tools

- Defined roles and accountability

- Integration with the SAP PM and review systems
Commonly Used RCA Tools

| Tool | Purpose | How It Is Used |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 5 Whys | Quickly find the root of simple problems | Ask "Why?" iteratively (usually five times) until the true cause is uncovered |
| Fishbone Diagram (Ishikawa) | Visualize contributing factors across categories (e.g., People, Methods, Machine) | Used during brainstorming to map all potential causes |
| Pareto Chart | Identify the most frequent failure modes or causes | Based on failure frequency or cost; helps prioritize what to address |

  o The team ensures operational sequencing aligns with safety, quality, and production requirements, especially during equipment start-ups or changeovers.

  o Regular maintenance of software and hardware components, troubleshooting, logic modifications, and firmware updates are carried out jointly or in coordination with maintenance teams.
